# Advanced Machine Learning -- Reinforcement Learning

## Activity-Centric Course Blueprint (1-1-1 Model)

### Course Information

-   **Course:** Advanced Machine Learning -- Reinforcement Learning
-   **Programme:** B.Tech CSE
-   **Semester:** VI
-   **Pattern:** 1-1-1 (Lecture + Tutorial + Lab)
-   **Duration:** 15 Weeks
-   **Contact Hours:** 45 (15L + 15T + 15P)

## Vision

Students progressively build an intelligent reinforcement learning agent
throughout the semester. Every activity contributes to a single evolving
GitHub repository.

## Course Outcomes

1.  Explain reinforcement learning concepts and formulate sequential
    decision problems.
2.  Model environments as Markov Decision Processes and evaluate
    policies.
3.  Design RL solutions using Dynamic Programming, Monte Carlo and
    Temporal Difference learning.
4.  Implement and evaluate Deep Reinforcement Learning algorithms using
    modern frameworks.
5.  Critically analyse AI-generated RL implementations and communicate
    design decisions.

# Activity Map 

## Module 1: Foundations of Reinforcement Learning (Weeks 1–2)

1.  #### Is Reinforcement Learning the Right Paradigm?; Driving Question: Why can't supervised or unsupervised learning solve every sequential decision problem?; Student Evidence: A comparative concept map, A one-page justification; Bloom Level: Analyze; Assessment: Rubric-based(conceptual accuracy, quality of reasoning, clarity of presentation); Feedback: Addressing Misconceptions, Highlighting strength in reasoning, suggesting improvements.
2.  #### Modeling the World as an RL Problem; Driving Question: Can every real-world problem be represented as a reinforcement learning problem?; Student Evidence: A formal RL representation of one real-world problem (e.g., autonomous driving, elevator scheduling, recommendation systems, traffic management), A one-page explanation justifying the choice of states, actions, rewards, and episode termination conditions; Bloom Level: Apply; Assessment: Rubric-based(Correct Indentification of RL components, Justification of modelling decisions, presentation Quality); Feedback: Modelling Assumptions and completeness.
3.  #### Investigating an Unknown Agent; Driving Question: Can we infer an agent's objective only by observing its behaviour?; Student Evidence: Observation log of at least 100 episodes of CartPole, Learning Journal describing behaviour patterns, A hypothesis explaining the agent;s objective and expected reward structure; Bloom Level: Analyze; Assessment: Rubric-based(Quality of observations, logical hypothesis formulation, evidence supporting conclusions); Feedback: Quality of observations, encouragement to refine their hypotheses
4.  #### Evaluating AI as a learning partner; Driving Question: Can we trust AI-generated explanations of Reinforcement Learning?; Student Evidence: An Annotated comparision between an AI-generated explanation and Sutton & Barto's explanation, Identification of at least five conceptual similarities or differences, Recommendation regarding the reliability of the AI explanation; Bloom Level: Evaluate; Assessment: Rubric-based(Identification of conceptual differences, Critical evaluation, Quality of Recommendation); Feedback: Common misconceptions identified by AI
5.  #### Constructing the Reinforcement Learning Knowledge Network; Driving Question: How are the fundamental concepts of Reinforcement learning connected?; Student Evidence: A concept network linking major RL concepts, Short explanations for every relationship shown; Bloom Level: Analyze; Assessment: Rubric-based(Completeness, Accuracy of Relationships, Visual Clarity); Feedback: Improving concept maps after peer discussion
6.  #### Builiding a Research Learning Portifolio; Driving Question: How can evidence collected throughout the semester demonstrate learning?; Student Evidence: Github respository, Portfolio template, reflection describing personal learning goals for the semester; Bloom Level: Create; Assessment: Rubric-based(Portfolio organization, Documentation quality, Reflection); Feedback: Repository Structure and suggestion for maintaining a professional learning portfolio

## Module 2: Markov Decision Processes (Weeks 3–4)

7.  #### Is the world really Markovian?; Driving Question: Do real-world decision-making problems satisfy the Markov Property?; Student Evidence: Analysis of two real-world systems, written argument discussing whether the Markov Property holds; Bloom Level: Analyze; Assessment: Rubric-based(Correct application of the Markov Property, Logical argumentation, clarity); Feedback: Alternative viewpoints and encourage revision
8.  #### Manual Policy Evaluation; Driving Question: Can policy values be computed without programming? Student Evidence: Complete Calculations, Comparision b/w manual calculations and software output, Reflection on observed differences; Bloom Level: Apply; Assessment: Rubric-based(Computational correctness, Interpretation of results, Reflection); Feeback: Mathematical Reasoning, Computational accuracy 
9.  #### Deriving the Bellman Equation; Driving Question: Why is the Bellman equation considered the foundation of Reinforcement Learning?; Student Evidence: Mathematical derivation, Annotated explanation of each derivation step, one-page summary explaining its significance; Bloom Level: Analyze; Assessment: Rubric-based(Mathematical correctness, quality of explanation, Presentation); Feedback: Comments on Correct/Incorrect derivation
10. #### Experiment Analysis of Policy Evaluation; Driving Question: How efficiently does iterative policy evaluation converge?; Student Evidence: Python Notebook, Convergence Plots, Discussion relating convergence to discount factors; Bloom Level: Analyze; Assessment: Rubric-based(Correct implementation, Experimental Analysis, Visualization quality); Feedback: Experimental design, encouragement to use different parameters
11. #### Verifying AI-generated Mathematics; Driving Question: Can AI correctly derive mathematical equations used in Reinforcement Learning?; Student Evidence: Annotated AI derivation, Error identification report, Corrected derivation with justification; Bloom Level: Evaluate; Assessment: Rubric-based(Error identification, correct mathematical reasoning, justification); Feedback: Common Mathematical misconceptions, importance of evidence-based verification
12. #### Which Policy performs best?; Driving Question: Does improving a policy always improve an agent's performance?; Student Evidence: Comparative experimental results, Performance graphs, Evidence-based conclusion identifying the best-performing policy with reason; Bloom Level: Evaluate; Assessment: Rubric-based(Experimental design, Interpretation of results, quality of conclusion); Feedback: Experimental Methodology, afterwards inclusion in sem portfolio 


##  Dynamic Programming (Weeks 5-6)

13. #### Why does value iteration converge?; Driving Question: Why does repeatedely updating state values eventually lead to an optimal policy?; Student Evidence: A visual representation of succesive value updates for a small GridWorld environment, A convergence graph showing value estimates across iterations, A one-page explanation describing why convergence occurs and the role of Bellman Optimality Equation; Bloom Level: Analyze; Assessment: Rubric-based(Accuracy of iterative value updates, Interpretation of convergence behaviour, Quality of visualization and explanation); Feedback: Understanding of convergence, discounting, and recursive updates
14. #### Discovering Dynamic Programming principles; Driving Question: What characterstics of a problem make Dynamic Programming possible?; Student Evidence: Classification of six computational problems as suitable/unsuitable for DP, A justification identifying the presence or absence of optimal substructure and overlapping subproblems, A summary describing the two fundamental principles underlying DP; Bloom Level: Analyze; Assessment: Rubric-based(Correct classification of problems, Quality of reasoning and justification, Clarity of summary); Feedback: Reasoning, common misconceptions, update portfolios
15. #### Comparing Policy Iteration and Value Iteration; Driving Question: Do Policcy iteration and Value iteration always produce the same optimal solution, and how do they differ computationally?; Student Evidence: Python implementations of policy and value iteration, Experimental comparision including execution time, number of iterations, and convergence plots, A comparative discussion identifying the advantages and limitations of each algorithm; Bloom Level: Evaluate; Assessment: Rubric-based(Correct implementation, Experimental comparision, Quality of Conclusions); Feedback: Experimental Methodology, Interpretation of Results, Importance of empirical evidence.
16. #### Designing better Rewards for FrozenLake; Driving Question: Can changing the reward structure produce better learning behaviour without changing the algorithm?; Student Evidence: Three alternative reward designs for FrozenLake, Experimental comparision of learning outcomes under each reward structure, graphical analysis of cumulative rewards and convergence, Evidence-based recommendation identifying the most effective reward design; Bloom Level: Create; Assessment: Rubric-based(Creativity and appropriateness of reward design, Experimental evaluation and analysis, Quality of Recommendation); Feedback: Relationship b/w reward engineering and agent behaviour, experimental design
17. #### Debugging a DP Agent; Driving Question: Can systematic debugging reveal why an intelligent agent fails to learn?; Student Evidence: A debugging report identifying all detected errors, Corrected source code with appropriate documentation, A brief explanation describing how each identified error affected the learning behaviour of the agent; Bloom Level: Evaluate; Assessment: Rubric-based(Identification of implementation errors, Quality of debugging and corrections, Explanation of algorithmic impact); Feedback: Debugging strategies, code quality, reasoning.
18. #### Reflecting on the limits of DP; Driving Question: If DP is optimal, why isn't it used to solve every Reinforcement Learning problem?; Student Evidence: A reflective essay discussing the limitations of DP, A comparision table contrasting DP with model-free reinforcement learning approaches, A concept map illustrating where DP fits within the broader RL landscape; Bloom Level: Evaluate; Assessment: Rubric-based(Conceptual understanding of DP limitations, Depth of critical reflection, Organization and clarity of presentation); Feedback: Individualized feedback highlighting the quality of students' disciplinary reasoning, and their ability to synthesize concepts across the module, Revision and incorporation of final version into sem learning portfolio as evidence of conceptual growth

## Monte Carlo Methods (Weeks 7-8) 

19. #### Can an Agent learn without knowing the environment?; Driving Question: Is it possible for an agent to learn an optimal policy without knowing the transition probabilities of the environment?; Student Evidence: A comparative concept map illustrating the differences betweem model-based and model=free reinforcement learning, A one-page explanation discussing why Monte Carlo methods eliminate the need for explicit transition models, Two real-world examples where Monto Carlo methods are more appropriate than DP; Bloom Level: Analyze; Assessment: Rubric-based(Conceptual understanding of model-free learning, Quality of comparative reasoning, Organization and presentation); Feedback: Conceptual understanding of model-free learning and common misconceptions regarding environment models
20. #### Estimating returns in Blackjack; Driving Question: How can an agent estimate the value of a decision using only experience?; Student Evidence: Manual return calculations for selected Blackjack episodes, Python notebook estimating state values from simulated episodes, Graph showing the convergence of estimated returns as the number of episodes increases, A brief discussion explaining the relationship between sampling and value estimation; Bloom Level: Apply; Assessment: Rubric-based(Accuracy of return estimation, Correct implementation and visualization, Interpretation of convergence); Feedback: Computational accuracy, Sources of Sampling Error, Compare their estimates with theoretical expectations
21. #### Which Monte Carlo strategy learns better?; Driving Question: Does First-Visit Monte Carlo always outperform Every-Visit Monte Carlo, or does the choice depend on the problem?; Student Evidence: Implementations of both Monte Carlo approaches, Comparative graphs showing convergence and value estimates, Experimental report discussing similarities, differences, and situations where one method may be preferred over the other; Bloom Level: Evaluate; Assessment: Rubric-based(Correct implementation of both algorithms, Quality of experimental comparision, Evidence-based conclusions); Feedback: Experimental methodology, Interpretation of results, Encouragement to justify conclusions using empirical evidence rather than intuition
22. #### Building a Monte Carlo Prediction Agent; Driving Question: Can an intelligent agent accurately estimate value functions through repeated interaction with its environment?; Student Evidence: Python implementation of Monte Carlo prediction, Experimental notebook documenting multiple simulation runs, Performance graphs illustrating value estimation over increasing episodes, Reflection discussing the strengths and limitations of Monte Carlo Prediction; Bloom Level: Create; Assessment: Rubric-based(Correct implementation, Experimental design and analysis, Reflection on algorithmic behaviour; Feedback: Implementation Quality, Experimental Design, and Interpretation of Results
23. #### Evaluating AI-generated Monte Carlo implementations; Driving Question: Can AI-generated reinforcement learning code be trusted without verification?; Student Evidence: Annotated AI-generated Monte Carlo code, Error analysis report identifying conceptual and programming mistakes, Corrected implementation with explanations for every modification, Reflection discussing the strengths and limitations of AI-assisted programming; Bloom Level: Evaluate; Assessment: Rubric-based(Identification of implementation errors, Quality of corrections and justifications, Reflection on responsible AI usage; Feedback: AI-generated misconceptions, Evidence-based verification practices
24. #### Peer Review of Monte Carlo investigations; Driving Question: Can scientific peer-review improve the quality and credibility of reinforcement learning experiments?; Student Evidence: A completed peer-review rubric evaluating another students' Monte Carlo Investigation, Written feedback highlighting strengths, weakness, and recommendations, a revised version of their own investigation incorporarting peer feedback, A short reflection describing how peer review influenced the quality of their work; Bloom Level: Evaluate; Assessment: Rubric-based(Quality and fairness of peer evaluation, Constructiveness of written feedback, Evidence of improvement in revised submission); Feedback: Peer-review moderation, Feedback quality validation, Additional Guidance 

## Temporal Difference Learning (Weeks 9-10) 

25. #### Can an agent learn before an episode ends?; Driving Question: Why should an intelligent agent wait until the end of an episode to learn from its experience?; Student Evidence: A comparative concept map illustrating the differences b/w Monte Carlo & Temporal Difference Learning, A one-page explanation describing the concept of bootstrapping and its role in TD learning, A real-world example where incremental learning is advantageous over episodic learning; Bloom Level: Analyze; Assessment: Rubric-based(Conceptual Understanding of TD learning, Quality of comparative reasoning, Organization and presentation; Feedback: Understanding of bootstrapping and online learning
26. #### Understanding Temporal Difference Error; Driving Question: How does an agent know whether its prediction was correct before reaching the final outcome?; Student Evidence: Manual TD(0) calculations for a sequence of state transitions, Step-by-step computation of TD errors and updated value estimations, A graphical illustration showing how prediction errors decrease over successive updates, A reflection describing the significance of TD error in RL; Bloom Level: Apply; Assessment: Rubric-based(Correct TD calculations, Interpretation of prediction errors, Quality of graphical representation); Feedback: Review on student calculations, Computational errors identification, Interpretation of TD error as a learning signal
27. #### Learning Safe Behaviour through SARSA; Driving Question: How does learning from the actions actually taken influence an agent's behaviour?; Student Evidence: Python implementation of the SARSA algorithm, Experimental results demonstrating learning over multiple episodes, Learning curves showing cumulative rewards, A discussion explaining why SARSA is considered an on-policy learning algorithm and identifying situations where it may be preferred; Bloom Level: Create; Assessment: Rubric-based(Correct implementation, Experimental analysis and interpretation, Quality of discussion); Feedback: Algorithm implementation, Encouragement of additional experimentation with exploration parameters, Interpretation of the behaviour of on-policy learning
28. #### Can an Agent Learn from the Best Possible Action?; Driving Question: Should an intelligent agent always learn as if it had chosen the best possible action?; Student Evidence: Python implementation of Q-learning, Experimental notebook comparing learning under different exploration rates, Learning curves illustrating cumulative rewards, A written explanation describing why Q-learning is classified as an off-policy learning algorithm; Bloom Level: Create; Assessment: Rubric-based(Correct implementation, Experimental investigation, Explanation of off-policy learning); Feedback: Implementation quality, Discussion on Exploration v/s Exploitation trade-offs, Hyperparameter-tuning
29. #### Which Learns Better—SARSA or Q-Learning?; Driving Question: Does one Temporal Difference algorithm consistently outperform the other, or does performance depend on the environment?; Student Evidence: Comparative implementations of SARSA and Q-learning, Experimental results across at least two reinforcement learning environments, Graphical comparison of cumulative rewards, convergence rates, and policy performance, An evidence-based report recommending which algorithm should be preferred under different environmental conditions; Bloom Level: Evaluate; Assessment: Rubric-based(Quality of experimental design, Interpretation of comparative results, Evidence-based recommendation); Feedback: Review on Experimental Methodology, Unsupported conclusions during class discussion
30. #### Designing and Defending an Intelligent Agent; Driving Question: Can experimental evidence justify why one reinforcement learning agent performs better than another?; Student Evidence: A fully functioning SARSA or Q-learning agent, Competition results obtained on a predefined benchmark environment, A performance analysis report explaining the impact of algorithm selection, exploration strategy, learning rate, and discount factor, A five-minute oral presentation defending the design choices made during development; Bloom Level: Evaluate; Assessment: Rubric-based(Performance of the implemented agent, Quality of evidence-based reasoning, Effectiveness of oral presentation and defence); Feedback: Individualized feedback on algorithm selection, experimental methodology and scientific communication.

## Deep RL (Weeks 11-12) 

31. #### Can Neural Networks Replace Lookup Tables?; Driving Question: How can an agent learn value functions when the state space becomes too large for tabular methods?; Student Evidence: Comparative analysis between tabular Q-learning and function approximation, A concept diagram illustrating how neural networks approximate Q-values, A one-page reflection on the limitations of tabular reinforcement learning; Bloom Level: Analyze; Assessment: Rubric-based(Conceptual understanding, Comparative reasoning, Clarity of presentation); Feedback: Students' understanding of function approximatio, Emphasis on RL's importance for High-dimensional state-spaces
32. #### Does Remembering the Past Improve Learning?; Driving Question: Why does storing past experiences improve the stability of Deep Q-Networks?; Student Evidence: Illustration of the Experience Replay mechanism, Experimental comparison of learning with and without Experience Replay, A short discussion explaining why randomly sampled experiences reduce learning instability; Bloom Level: Analyze; Assessment: Rubric-based(Understanding of Experience Replay, Experimental interpretation, Quality of explanation); Feedback: Correlated experiences and unstable learning, experimental interpretations
33. #### Building a Deep Q-Network Agent; Driving Question: Can a neural network learn an optimal action-value function directly from interaction with the environment?; Student Evidence: Python implementation of a Deep Q-Network, Experimental notebook documenting training progress, Learning curves showing cumulative rewards and episode lengths, Brief report discussing implementation challenges and observed behaviour; Bloom Level: Create; Assessment: Rubric-based(Correct implementation, Experimental analysis, Documentation and discussion); Feedback: Review on implementation quality, coding practices, and experimental methodology
34. #### Investigating Why Deep Q-Networks Become Unstable; Driving Question: Why do Deep Q-Networks sometimes fail to converge even when the algorithm is implemented correctly?; Student Evidence: Debugging report identifying possible causes of instability, Experimental evidence supporting each identified cause, Revised implementation incorporating corrective measures, Reflection explaining the impact of each modification; Bloom Level: Evaluate; Assessment: Rubric-based(Identification of instability sources, Quality of debugging and evidence, Reflection); Feedback: Justification of every modification using experimental evidence
35. #### Can AI Generate Reliable Deep Reinforcement Learning Code?; Driving Question: Should AI-generated Deep Reinforcement Learning implementations be accepted without verification?; Student Evidence: Annotated AI-generated DQN implementation, Error analysis identifying conceptual, architectural, or coding mistakes, Corrected implementation with justification for each modification, Reflection on the responsible use of AI-assisted programming; Bloom Level: Evaluate; Assessment: Rubric-based(Identification of implementation errors, Quality of corrections and justification, Reflection on responsible AI use); Feedback: Common AI-generated errors in RL implementations, importance of evidence-based verification before deploying AI-generated code
36. #### Can Better Hyperparameters Produce Better Intelligence?; Driving Question: How do hyperparameters influence the learning efficiency and performance of a Deep Q-Network?; Student Evidence: Experimental study investigating at least three hyperparameters (e.g., learning rate, batch size, replay buffer size, target network update frequency, ε-decay), Comparative performance graphs, Evidence-based recommendation of an effective hyperparameter configuration, Reflection discussing the trade-offs observed during experimentation; Bloom Level: Evaluate; Assessment: Rubric-based(Experimental design, Analysis of results, Evidence-based recommendations); Feedback: Experimental design, statistical interpretation, and scientific reporting, justification of hyperparameter choices

## Policy Gradient (Weeks 13-14)

37. #### Why optimize policies directly?; Driving Question: Why learn a value function first when an agent can learn the optimal policy directly?; Student Evidence: Derivation of the Policy Gradient Theorem with annotated explanations, A concept map comparing value-based and policy-based reinforcement learning, A one-page reflection explaining the advantages and limitations of direct policy optimization; Bloom Level: Analyze; Assessment: Rubric-based(Correctness of derivation, Conceptual understanding and reasoning, Clarity of presentation); Feedback: Mathematical Reasoning, common misconceptions in policy optimization, addition to student portfolio
38. #### Can an Agent Learn a Policy Through Experience?; Driving Question: Can an agent learn an optimal policy directly by interacting with its environment without learning an explicit value function?; Student Evidence: Python implementation of the REINFORCE algorithm, Experimental notebook documenting training progress, Learning curves showing cumulative rewards across episodes, A brief report discussing convergence behaviour and implementation challenges; Bloom Level: Create; Assessment: Rubric-based(Correct implementation, Experimental analysis, Quality of documentation); Feedback: Review on implementation quality, experimental methodology, interpretation of results.
39. #### Can Two Neural Networks Learn Better Than One?; Driving Question: How does separating decision-making from value estimation improve reinforcement learning?; Student Evidence: Python implementation of an Actor-Critic algorithm, Experimental comparison with the REINFORCE algorithm, Performance graphs illustrating convergence and cumulative rewards, A reflection discussing the interaction between the Actor and Critic components; Bloom Level: Create; Assessment: Rubric-based(Correct implementation, Experimental comparison, Reflection on Actor-Critic architecture); Feedback: Implementation, algorithmic understanding, interpretation of experimental findings
40. #### Is Proximal Policy Optimization Worth the Complexity?; Driving Question: Why has Proximal Policy Optimization (PPO) become one of the most widely adopted reinforcement learning algorithms?; Student Evidence: A seminar presentation explaining the core ideas behind PPO, A comparative analysis of PPO, REINFORCE, and Actor-Critic, A summary discussing the advantages and limitations of PPO based on current literature; Bloom Level: Evaluate; Assessment: Rubric-based(Technical accuracy, Comparative analysis, Presentation effectiveness); Feedback: Technical communication, conceptual understanding, and students' ability to explain advanced RL algos to broader audience
41. #### What Makes Reinforcement Learning Research Credible?; Driving Question: How do researchers evaluate and communicate new ideas in Reinforcement Learning?; Student Evidence: Critical review of a recent Reinforcement Learning research paper, Identification of the research problem, methodology, experimental design, and key findings, A reflective critique discussing the strengths, limitations, and potential future directions of the work; Bloom Level: Evaluate; Assessment: Rubric-based(Understanding of the research contribution, Critical analysis, Quality of written review); Feedback: Critical Reading Skills, encouragement of evidence-based critiques
42. #### Which Reinforcement Learning Algorithm Performs Best?; Driving Question: Does a single reinforcement learning algorithm consistently outperform all others across different environments?; Student Evidence: Comparative benchmarking of at least three reinforcement learning algorithms studied during the course, Performance metrics including cumulative reward, convergence rate, training stability, and computational cost, Comparative visualizations and tables, An evidence-based report recommending appropriate algorithms for different classes of reinforcement learning problems; Bloom Level: Evaluate; Assessment: Rubric-based(Experimental design, Comparative analysis and interpretation, Evidence-based recommendations); Feedback: Benchmarking Methodology, Statistical interpretation, scientific reporting, Students refine their comparative analysis before incorporating it into their semester learning portfolio as a synthesis of the techniques studied throughout the course

## Capstone (Week 15)

43. #### Can You Design an Intelligent Agent for a Real-World Problem?; Driving Question: Can the reinforcement learning concepts and algorithms studied throughout the course be integrated to solve a real-world sequential decision-making problem?; Student Evidence: A complete Reinforcement Learning solution addressing a real-world problem (e.g., autonomous navigation, resource allocation, inventory management, game AI, recommendation systems, etc.), Source code and project repository, Experimental evaluation including performance metrics and visualizations, A technical report describing the problem formulation, algorithm selection, implementation, experimental results, and limitations; Bloom Level: Create; Assessment: Rubric-based(Problem formulation and algorithm selection, Implementation and experimental evaluation, Technical documentation and presentation); Feedback: Technical implementation, experimental methodology, and scientific communication
44. #### Can You Defend Your Intelligent Agent?; Driving Question: How effectively can you communicate and justify the design decisions behind your reinforcement learning solution?; Student Evidence: A live demonstration of the implemented reinforcement learning system, A 10-minute presentation explaining the problem, methodology, experimental findings, and challenges encountered, Completed peer evaluation forms assessing another team's project, A brief reflection summarizing insights gained from peer presentations; Bloom Level: Evaluate; Assessment: Rubric-based(Effectiveness of demonstration, Quality of technical explanation and defence, Constructive peer evaluation and reflection); Feedback: Peers also provide structured feedback on techincal quality, presentation skills, and evidence supporting design decisions, Students use this feedback to strengthen their final portfolio submission
45. #### How Has Your Understanding of Intelligent Learning Evolved?; Driving Question: How has your understanding of Reinforcement Learning and intelligent decision-making evolved throughout this course?; Student Evidence: A complete semester learning portfolio containing all revised Learning Investigations, A reflective essay (1000–1200 words) describing conceptual growth, challenges encountered, key learning experiences, and future learning goals, Participation in an individual viva voce defending the portfolio, project decisions, and understanding of core reinforcement learning concepts; Bloom Level: Create; Assessment: Rubric-based(Completeness and quality of learning portfolio, Depth of reflection and synthesis, Viva performance demonstrating conceptual understanding); Feedback: Student's conceptual developement, technical competence, and reflective growth across the sem, the completed portfolio serves as a lasting record of the student's evidence-based learning journey, and provides guidance for future study and professional practice
