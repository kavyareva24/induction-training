That is a provocative and very real perspective to start with. In many ways, the traditional system operates exactly like that—a pipeline producing specialized inputs for the corporate machine. 

Let's unpack this assumption:

1. If we view education primarily as a mechanism to supply labor for companies, **what does that imply about the role of the teacher and the agency of the student?** Are we facilitators of growth, or quality control inspectors on an assembly line?
2. In a rapidly evolving field like Computer Science—where libraries, languages, and AI tooling shift every few months—**how does this "labor supply" model hold up?** If we only train students for the immediate needs of a company today, how do we prepare them for the day those technical stacks become obsolete?
3. If a student is merely "labor in training," **how do we design learning experiences that go beyond passive compliance and rote performance to cultivate genuine critical thinking and problem-solving?**
